The news crossed the continent faster than any dirigible: the Ash Throne, held by one unbroken bloodline for over two thousand years, belongs to a commoner. Arven Deymar — no noble blood, no famous regiments behind him — took the Empire of Ashes in a coup so unexpected that foreign courts spent weeks refusing to believe the dispatches. How exactly he did it is already half legend and half state secret; a weak emperor, a nobility at war with itself over the succession, and a rising caste of engineers and officers with no love for inherited rank all played their part.

His enemies call him the Usurper. His soldiers call him Emperor and mean it. Deymar preaches merit over blood with a convert's sincerity and a hangman's consistency — he has been as ruthless with incompetent allies as with highborn enemies.

The old families have not surrendered, merely gone quiet. Every power on Myria is watching to see whether the idea he embodies can survive — and what he will do with the great guns when they arrive.
