A minor curiosity of the antiquarian trade. A handful of copies exist, scattered and ill-printed, of a memoir by an unnamed beggar who claimed that, in the ruined years of the deep past, a smiling stranger sat with him in the mud, gave him coin, told him the true history of the world — and then staked his own existence on a game of dice. The beggar won, the stranger thanked him and came apart "like dust deciding to rest," and the beggar sobered up for the only time in his life to write it all down.

No serious scholar treats it as anything but invention; the few historians who have held a copy note only that the copies are older than they ought to be, and turn up in places they shouldn't.

Collectors pay modestly for it. Once in a long while, somebody pays far too much.
