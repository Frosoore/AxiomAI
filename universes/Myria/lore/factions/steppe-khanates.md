The eastern steppe belongs to the Khanates — nomad clans unified under a being the settled nations barely know how to name. Generations ago, a defeated chief, his army destroyed by a High-Ports expedition, stood his ground alone; the land itself split open at his feet, and he rose from the crack changed. He drove the invaders into the sea and bound the clans to him — not as a king, but as something the steppe-folk say *is* the people, the way a river is its water.

The God-Khan strengthens his riders, and his existence quietly alarms every chancellery on Myria — not as the return of anything old, but as something entirely new and entirely misunderstood.

Khanate life is deliberately traditional: the bond, their shamans say, demands it. Their shamanism resembles no settled school of magic in either form or effect, and the Scientific Coalition would pay almost anything to study it. The clans also keep relics from a half-buried bunker — devices they use faithfully and do not pretend to understand.
