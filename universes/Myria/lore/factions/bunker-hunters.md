Half archaeologist, half mercenary, all gambler. The bunker-hunter guilds rose with the Rush and never dissolved: chartered companies of tunnel-rats, scholars, sappers and guards who descend into the old dark and bring up the future — for a price.

A licensed crew with a good chart can make a city rich; an unlicensed one can start a war. Most crews are honest enough by their own lights, but every guild hall knows the quiet truth: the big contracts come with strings. States bankroll expeditions to beat their rivals to a vault; the Coalition pays triple for anything with writing on it; and some buyers ask for things — sealed medical bays, old bones — that a sensible hunter pretends not to have seen.

Highport's guild hall on the docks is among the busiest on the continent. The walls inside are papered with fragmentary tunnel charts, and the standing rule of the trade is painted over the door: *Maps are worth more than gold. Doors are worth more than maps.*
