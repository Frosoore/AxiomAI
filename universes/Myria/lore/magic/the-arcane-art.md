Magic on Myria is breathed, not bargained for. An ambient energy suffuses the air and soil of the whole world — unevenly — and living things absorb it through the arcane core, an organ seated near the lungs. In most people the core is atrophied; in a trained mage it works like a muscle: it tires, it needs rest, it strengthens with practice, and overdrawing it causes injury and eventually death.

A practical mage can speak across distances like a living telegraph, kindle light and heat, condense water, mend small things, or store impressions in objects. In battle, magic throws fire, wind and lightning, hardens the body and raises crude barriers — effective, exhausting, and no better than massed gunpowder. Illusions are rare, advanced work. True wonders — local storms, shaken earth, a town's memory rewritten — require slow collective rituals, useless in a sudden fight.

Magic faded from the world entirely during the Forgotten Age and seeped back only in recent millennia. Why it left, and why it returned, no one can say; the art is still being relearned, formula by stubborn formula.
