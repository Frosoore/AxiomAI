Steam hisses from the brass valves of the harbour cranes as another freighter noses into Highport, its paddle-wheels churning the grey water white. Above the rooftops, a Coalition courier dirigible drifts toward the Upper Terraces, indifferent to the customs flags snapping below.

You are in the Gilded Compass, a dockside tavern wedged between a chandlery and a pawnshop, where the gin is cheap and nobody asks twice about a name. The morning crowd is thin: two dockers arguing over a wage chit, a tired clerk of the Council nursing tea, and a grizzled bunker hunter by the window, her coat still dusted with tunnel chalk, a sealed expedition contract on the table in front of her.

Behind the bar, Maela polishes a glass and watches the street. Word in the harbour is the same word as everywhere these days: the Empire's great guns are nearly ready, the Council of Captains is nervous, and knowledge buys more than gold.

The city smells of coal smoke, salt and possibility. What do you do?

Rain drums on the skylights of Highport's public reading-house, where crystal lamps burn steady over rows of scarred oak desks. Scholars, clerks and the occasional foreign agent bend over ledgers and maps; in the Scholars' Quarter, paper is power.

You have a desk near the stacks, and trouble finding what you came for. The catalogue lists a memoir — an old curiosity about a beggar who claimed he diced with a god — but the shelf where it should stand is empty, and the assistant librarian only shrugs: "Sold, maybe. There's a translator down on Cog Street who buys odd lots. Veyran, I think. Calder Veyran."

Outside, newsboys are shouting about the Empire again — a usurper on the Ash Throne, great guns in the forges, captains meeting behind closed doors. The rain shows no sign of stopping.
