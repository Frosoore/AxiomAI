Some three centuries ago, underground explosions tore across a huge stretch of the continent. The blasts cracked open tunnels leading to ancient bunkers — deep, intact, stocked with machines, archives and sciences no living tradition remembered. Nobody had known they existed. Nobody knows for certain who built them, or why they failed all at once.

For about eighty years people were too frightened to go down. Then came the Bunker Rush: official expeditions, guilds of bunker hunters, fortunes made overnight, wars nearly started over a single sealed door. Everything that defines the present age — steam engines, vaccines, the return of disciplined magic, the rise of the Scientific Coalition — flows from what was carried up out of the dark.

The hunger has never slackened. A single untouched bunker can overturn the balance of nations, and every power knows it.
